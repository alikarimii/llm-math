# Where the Numbers Come From

<div className="lede">
You have written this, or something like it:

`temperature: 0.7`

This page is about what it actually does — and to explain that, we first have to
explain what it is doing it *to*. Every number here comes from the same
transformer we trained in the last lesson. Nothing is staged for effect.
</div>

<ProbabilityScrollStage>

<Step label="the residual stream">
## The last lesson stopped one step early

At the end of *[How a word decides what to look at](/attention)*, the model had
just finished reading a sentence, and every word had walked away with sixteen
numbers. That was the whole payoff: **it** had looked back at **dog**, and the
row for **it** carried the evidence.

Then the page ended, and the model never said anything.

Look at the row on the left. It is the last word's row — sixteen numbers, the
same shape the sentence arrived in. That row has a name, and it is the only piece
of jargon in this section: the **residual stream**. It means exactly what you are
looking at — the running sixteen-number summary each word carries through the
model, which every layer reads from and adds its findings back into, so that it
comes out the same size it went in. One layer of attention has already written to
it. Now something has to read it.

It is not an answer. It is not a word, or a
ranking, or a probability. There is nothing in it you could print. To get from
this row to *the next word*, the model needs one more piece of machinery, and it
has been sitting in the weights file this entire time.
</Step>

<Step label="unembed">
## One more matrix

Here it is. Sixteen rows of sixteen numbers, called **unembed**, and it has
shipped in `weights.json` since the first day of this project without a single
figure ever drawing it.

Its shape is the tell. The model's vocabulary is sixteen words — `the`, `cat`,
`dog`, `bird`, `drank`, `ate`, `found`, `quickly`, and eight more — and this
matrix has exactly one row per word. Each row is a sixteen-number question of
the form *how much does this look like me?*

So you take the last word's row — the sixteen numbers from the step before — and
you compare it against every row of this matrix, one dot product per word.
Sixteen comparisons in, sixteen scores out. One score per word in the language
the model knows.
</Step>

<Step label="logits">
## Sixteen scores, one per word

Those sixteen scores are what comes out, and they are the model's entire opinion
about what happens next.

We are asking it a specific question now. The context is just two words — **the
cat** — and the model is scoring every word it knows as a continuation. Four
scores come back positive and clustered at the top:

`quickly` **3.767** · `ate` **2.550** · `found` **2.452** · `drank` **2.375**

The other twelve come back negative. `it` scores **−3.164**, and the lowest of the
sixteen, `food`, scores **−7.725**.

Sixteen bars will not fit on the left, so the figure draws six: those four, plus
`it` and `bird`, the two best of the twelve. Tallest first, with the two negative
scores flat on the floor — which is a preview of something we come back to.

<Aside kind="primer" title="What is a logit?">
A **logit** is a raw score, before it means anything. It is not a probability
and does not pretend to be one: it can be negative, it has no upper limit, and
the sixteen of them do not add up to anything in particular. Their only job is
to be *comparable* — bigger means the model likes that word more here. Any
number you see coming out of a language model's final matrix, before it has been
normalised, is a logit.
</Aside>
</Step>

<Step label="softmax">
## Scores become probabilities

Watch the bars change shape. That is one function running: **softmax**, the same
four lines of code that turned attention scores into attention weights in the
previous lesson, doing the same job one last time.

It raises *e* to the power of each score and divides each result by the total.
Big scores get exponentially bigger, negative scores get squashed toward nothing,
and — because everything was divided by the total — the sixteen results are
guaranteed to sum to exactly 1.

`quickly` **0.551** · `ate` **0.163** · `found` **0.148** · `drank` **0.137**

That is a probability distribution over the next word, and it is the only thing
a language model ever produces. Not a word. Not a sentence. A list of numbers
that sums to one, and then — the part everyone forgets — a *die roll*.

So: the model thinks **the cat** is followed by `quickly` about 55% of the time,
and by one of three verbs the rest of the time. A reasonable-looking claim. But
why should you believe those particular numbers? Where does **0.551** come from?
</Step>

<Step label="count the corpus">
## Now go and count the training data

Stop looking at the network. Go and look at the eighteen sentences it was trained
on.

Six of them begin with **the cat**. Here they are, all of them:

- the cat **drank** water so it purred
- the cat **ate** bread so it purred
- the cat **found** food so it purred
- the cat **quickly** drank water so it purred
- the cat **quickly** ate bread so it purred
- the cat **quickly** found food so it purred

Now count the third word. `quickly` appears three times out of six. `drank`,
`ate`, and `found` appear once each. Written as fractions:

`quickly` **0.500** · `ate` **0.167** · `found` **0.167** · `drank` **0.167**

And the model — which has never counted anything, which arrived at its numbers by
multiplying matrices — said **0.551** · **0.163** · **0.148** · **0.137**.

That is the whole lesson, and everything after this is a consequence of it. Those
are the same numbers. The grey bars behind the model's are the counts; the model
landed on them. Nobody told it the counts. Nobody could have — there is no table
of frequencies anywhere in `weights.json`, only matrices. It learned them by
guessing, being told it was wrong, and being nudged, six thousand times.

<Aside kind="primer" title="What is a corpus?">
The **corpus** is just the training data: the complete set of text the model was
shown, and the only text it has ever seen. For a production model that is a
sizeable fraction of the internet, which is why nobody can ever quite tell you
what is in it. For this one it is eighteen hand-written sentences about a cat, a
dog, and a bird — the whole universe, listed above, small enough to count on
your fingers. That is the point: here you can check the claim rather than
believe it.
</Aside>
</Step>

<Step label="cross-entropy">
## The force that put those numbers there

The nudging has a name. It is **cross-entropy loss**, and it is one expression:

<Equation tex="\mathcal{L} = -\log P(\text{correct word})" display />

Take the model's probability for the word that actually came next, take its
logarithm, flip the sign. If the model gave the right word a probability of 1,
the loss is 0 — nothing to fix. As its probability for the right word falls
toward 0, the loss climbs without limit. Training does one thing: it changes
every number in the model, a little, in whichever direction makes this quantity
smaller. Six thousand times.

Here is what that produced. Score the three numbers on one context — **the cat**,
the one we have been counting all page — and compare them.

A model that knows nothing, one that spreads its probability evenly over all
sixteen words, scores **2.7726**, a **perplexity of 16.00**. It is choosing
between sixteen words, blind. Our trained model, after **the cat**, scores
**1.2506**, a **perplexity of 3.49**.

And the floor — the best score any model could possibly achieve *after those two
words*, even a perfect one — is **1.2425**, a **perplexity of 3.46**. (These are
all scores at one context. Across the whole corpus the model does considerably
better, because most contexts are not ambiguous at all: after **the cat drank**
there is only ever one word that can come next.)

We are three hundredths of a perplexity point away from perfect. And the model
cannot do better, ever, no matter how long you train it: after **the cat**, the
corpus genuinely *is* ambiguous. Four words really can come next. A model that
claimed certainty there would be wrong.

<Aside kind="primer" title="Why a logarithm?">
This is the one genuinely unfamiliar piece of mathematics on the page, and it
earns its place. The probability of a whole sentence is the probability of each
word *multiplied* together — and multiplying thousands of numbers smaller than 1
gives you a number so small it underflows to zero, and a gradient you cannot
work with.

A logarithm turns multiplication into addition. `log(a × b) = log(a) + log(b)`.
So "multiply all these probabilities" becomes "add up all these penalties",
which is numerically stable and pleasant to differentiate. The minus sign is
there because the log of a probability is always negative, and a loss you are
trying to *minimise* is easier to think about when it is positive. That is the
entire reason loss is written <Equation tex="-\log P" /> and not just
<Equation tex="P" />.

And that is where **nats** come from. A nat is simply the unit you get when the
logarithm you took was the natural one — base *e*, the `Math.log` in the code.
Use base 2 instead and the same quantity comes out in **bits**; it is the same
measurement in different money. So a loss of `2.7726 nats` just means *the
natural log of 16*, and `0.0082 nats` is a very small amount of the same stuff.
Nothing more is hiding in the word.
</Aside>

<Aside kind="primer" title="What is perplexity? What is KL?">
**Perplexity** is cross-entropy made readable: it is *e* raised to the loss, and
it answers the question *how many words is the model effectively choosing
between?* A perplexity of 16 on a 16-word vocabulary means the model is
choosing blindly among all of them — it knows nothing. A perplexity of 1 means
it is certain. Our **3.49 after the cat** means that, at that one context, the
model is choosing between about three and a half words. Given that four words
can legally follow **the cat**, that is roughly the truth. Ask it after **the cat
drank**, where only `water` ever follows, and the number drops close to 1 — a
perplexity is always a perplexity *somewhere*.

**KL divergence** sounds like a third thing to learn, and it is not. It is the
gap between the two numbers already on this page — the model's **1.2506** and the
floor's **1.2425** — and that gap is **0.008184 nats**. That is all KL is: *how
much worse than the best possible score you are*. It needs no formula of its own.
A KL of 0 would mean the model's distribution is exactly the corpus's
frequencies. Ours is 0.008 away from that, which is another way of saying: the
claim in the previous step is not poetry, it is measurable, and this is the
measurement. A test in this repo computes it on the six contexts this lesson
leans on and fails the build if any of them exceeds 0.05.
</Aside>
</Step>

<Step label="the cliff">
## It also learned which words are illegal

The model did not only learn the odds. Look at where the rest of its probability
went, or rather did not.

Those four words hold **99.84%** of the mass between them. The other twelve
words share the remaining **0.16%** — for all sixteen of them together to sum to
one, that is what is left. And the drop is not a slope, it is a cliff. `drank`,
the least likely of the four legal words, sits at a logit of **2.375**. The very
next word down, `it`, sits at **−3.164**.

Nothing lives in between. There is no word the model considers *somewhat*
plausible after **the cat**. It has learned that four words are allowed here and
twelve are not, and it has learned that far more emphatically than it learned
the odds among the four.

That fact is the reason the last knob in this lesson exists.

<Aside kind="deep" title="Why the tail matters on a real model">
Here the tail is twelve words holding 0.16%, and you could delete it without
anyone noticing. On a model with a 50,000-token vocabulary the tail is tens of
thousands of tokens, and it is much fatter — a few percent of the probability
mass, spread so thinly that no individual token matters, but collectively large
enough that a sampler *will* reach into it, and eventually pull out something
absurd. That is what `top_p` is really for: not to improve the top of the
distribution, which is fine, but to amputate a tail that is too long to see.
</Aside>
</Step>

<Step label="temperature down">
## Now you can lie to it

You know what the honest distribution is. You counted it. So now we can look at
what the knobs do to it, and — for once — say whether they made it better or
worse.

**Temperature is a division.** That is the whole of it: before softmax, divide
every logit by *T*.

<Equation tex="\text{softmax}(\text{logits} / T)" display />

Dividing by a number smaller than 1 makes every logit bigger, which stretches the
gaps between them, which softmax then exaggerates. Watch the bars as you scroll:
at `T = 0.25`, `quickly` climbs to **0.984**.

The model now claims it is 98% sure. But you *counted the corpus*. `quickly`
follows **the cat** three times in six. The truth is **0.500**.

Low temperature made the model **more confident and less truthful at once** —
and you can only see that because you already know what the truth was. This is
the sharp edge of the whole page. The confident-sounding output is the wrong one.

<Aside kind="deep" title="What happens at T = 0?">
A division by zero, strictly. But watch what happens as *T* gets smaller and
smaller: the biggest logit runs away from the others, and softmax gives it more
and more of the mass, until in the limit it has all of it and everything else has
none. That limit has a name — **greedy decoding**: always take the likeliest
word, roll no dice at all. It is what `temperature: 0` means in an API, and it is
implemented as a special case, not as an actual division. The slider below stops
at 0.1 for the same reason.
</Aside>
</Step>

<Step label="temperature up">
## And the other direction

Divide by a number *larger* than 1 and the gaps between the logits shrink instead.
Softmax sees four scores that look nearly the same, and it does the only honest
thing it can with four nearly-equal scores: it spreads the mass out.

At `T = 4.0`:

`quickly` **0.224** · `ate` **0.165** · `found` **0.161** · `drank` **0.158**

Among the four legal words, that is very nearly a coin flip: the model has given
up having an opinion about which one comes next.

But add those four numbers up. They come to **0.708**. At `T = 1.0` the same four
held **0.998**. So **29%** of the model's probability has gone somewhere else —
onto the twelve words that never once followed **the cat** in the training data.
`it` is now at **0.040**. `bird` is at **0.033**. Words the model had learned were
illegal here are back on the table, and a die roll will land on one of them
roughly once every three or four words.

That is the part the word "creativity" hides. High temperature does not just
flatten the top of the distribution. **It reopens the tail.** The cliff the last
step made so much of — four words at 99.84%, twelve at 0.16% — is not a fact about
the model, it is a fact about the model *at T = 1*. Divide the logits by four and
the cliff erodes into a slope. Push *T* higher still and every distribution
converges on the same thing: uniform, perplexity 16, the model that knows nothing.

So temperature does not add creativity or subtract it. It scales how far the
model's own scores are allowed to be from each other, and everything you have
heard about it is downstream of that — including the failure mode. What you
actually wanted, most likely, was the top of the distribution loosened and the
tail left shut. Temperature cannot give you that. It only has the one dial, and it
turns both at once.

Which is exactly why there is a second knob.
</Step>

<Step label="top-p">
## Truncating instead of reshaping

The other knob you have typed does something completely different. Temperature
*reshapes* the whole distribution, tail and all. **Top-p** — nucleus sampling —
leaves the shape alone and simply cuts it off. It is the knob that shuts the
tail temperature just opened, which is why the two are almost always set
together.

Sort the words by probability, add them up from the top, and stop as soon as the
running total reaches *p*. Keep those words, throw the rest away, and divide what
is left by its own total so it sums to 1 again. Everything below the line becomes
literally impossible.

On this model, with this context, the results are almost funny:

`p = 0.5` keeps **one** word. `p = 0.9` keeps **four**. And `p = 0.99` still
keeps **four** — because there is nothing in between to keep.

That is the cliff again. Between four words at 99.84% and twelve words at 0.16%,
almost any *p* you choose lands in the same place. Nucleus sampling on this model
is a blunt instrument because this model's opinion is blunt; on a real one, with
a real tail, that same rule is what stops the tenth-thousandth-most-likely token
from ever getting its turn.
</Step>

</ProbabilityScrollStage>

## Roll the dice yourself

One thing is still missing, and it is the thing that surprises people most: **the
distribution does not pick a word.**

It cannot. It is a list of numbers. Picking is a separate act, and it is the
crudest piece of code in this entire project — take a random number between 0 and
1, walk down the list adding probabilities up, and stop when the running total
passes it. That is `sampleFrom`, and that is all sampling is. A weighted die.

Which means a word with probability 0.5 comes up about half the time. Press the
button six times: you should get `quickly` around three of them, and one of the
verbs the rest — which is exactly what the six sentences said, at the top of this
page, when we counted them by hand.

## And that is the whole machine

There is nothing else. A language model reads a context, produces one number per
word in its vocabulary, softmaxes those numbers into a distribution, and rolls a
weighted die. Then it appends the word it drew, and does it all again. That is
what the model behind your API key is doing, on every token, forever. Its
vocabulary runs to tens of thousands of tokens rather than sixteen words, and it
stacks many layers rather than one — but the last matrix, the softmax, and the
die are these.

You came here to find out what `temperature: 0.7` does.

It divides. It divides the sixteen scores that come out of the last matrix, before
softmax turns them into odds — and you have now watched where every one of those
scores comes from, seen that the odds are the frequencies of what actually
followed in the training data, and seen exactly what you give up when you push
them around. At 0.7 you are asking the model to be a little more confident than
the evidence it was trained on actually supports.

Sometimes that is what you want. Now you know what it costs.
