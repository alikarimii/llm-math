<Step label="X = E + P">
## A sentence becomes numbers

Six words go in. The model doesn't see words — it sees six rows of sixteen
numbers each, one row per word, short enough that you can read a whole row
at a glance. Each row starts as a fixed embedding for that word, and a
second fixed vector for *where* the word sits in the sentence is added on
top, so `dog` in position two ends up as a slightly different row than `dog`
would be in position five.

<Aside kind="primer" title="What is a vector, really?">
Strip away the geometry and a vector is just an ordered list of numbers —
here, sixteen of them. "Sixteen-dimensional" sounds exotic, but you already
know how to read a list of sixteen numbers; the word just gives the list
a job.
</Aside>
</Step>

<Step label="Q, K, V = X·W">
## The same word, asked three different questions

Each of those rows gets multiplied by three different matrices —
<Equation tex="W_Q" />, <Equation tex="W_K" />, and <Equation tex="W_V" /> —
producing three new vectors per word: a **query**, a **key**, and a
**value**. Same sixteen input numbers, three different lenses on them.

The query is the word asking a question: *what am I looking for?* The key
is the word's answer to everyone else's question: *what do I offer?* The
value is what the word actually hands over once it's been found: *what I'll
pass on.* A word never compares its query to its own key on equal footing
with anyone else's — every word asks, and every word answers, at once.

Each of those matrices is sixteen by sixteen, so each still hands back
sixteen numbers per word. But those sixteen are then cut cleanly in half.
This model runs **two heads**, and each head is given its own
eight-number slice of every query, key, and value — the first head takes
the first eight components, the second head takes the last eight. So from
here on, every vector you see is **eight** numbers long, not sixteen, and
everything that follows happens inside the first of the two heads: the one
that learned to resolve the pronoun.

<Aside kind="primer" title="Why cut the vector into heads at all?">
A single head can only learn one notion of *relevant*, because it has only
one query-key comparison to spend. Splitting the sixteen dimensions into
two independent eight-dimensional slices buys a second, entirely separate
notion of relevance for the same arithmetic — the two heads look for
different things, at once, and neither can see the other's dimensions. The
width of a head is written <Equation tex="d_k" />, and here
<Equation tex="d_k = 16 / 2 = 8" />.
</Aside>
</Step>

<Step label="q · k">
## One query meets one key

Here is the only arithmetic on this page you could do yourself, with a
pencil, in under a minute. The word **it** is holding out its query, looking
for whatever might be its antecedent. The word **dog** is holding out its
key, offering itself as a candidate. To find out how well they match, you
multiply the two vectors element by element and add up the results — eight
products, one sum.

<Aside kind="primer" title="Why does a dot product measure similarity?">
Multiply two vectors' matching components and add up the results, and the
total is largest when the vectors point in the same direction, zero when
they're at right angles, and negative when they point apart. A dot product
is, quite literally, a measurement of how much two vectors agree.
</Aside>
</Step>

<Step label="QKᵀ">
## Every query, every key

The model doesn't only compare **it** to **dog** — it compares every word's
query to every word's key, six against six, thirty-six dot products in this
head. The result is a square grid of raw scores: entry *(i, j)* says how
strongly word *i*'s query matches word *j*'s key, before anything has been
decided.
</Step>

<Step label="÷ √dₖ">
## Divide by the square root of the dimension

<Equation tex="\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}" display />

Every score in the grid is divided by the same number: the square root of
the key dimension. On its own this step looks like housekeeping, but skip
it and the next step — softmax — stops behaving the way you'd expect.

<Aside kind="deep" title="Why √dₖ and not dₖ, or nothing at all?">
Suppose the components of a query and a key vector are each independent,
with mean zero and variance one — a reasonable approximation once a model
is trained. Their dot product sums <Equation tex="d_k" /> such
products, and because the terms are independent, variances add: the dot
product itself ends up with variance <Equation tex="d_k" />, not 1.

That matters because softmax is not scale-invariant. Feed it scores with
variance 1 and it produces a fairly soft distribution; feed it the same
scores stretched out by a growing variance and it saturates, collapsing
almost all its mass onto whichever entry happened to be largest — a fact
about softmax you'll see demonstrated directly in the first playground
below. Dividing by <Equation tex="\sqrt{d_k}" /> exactly cancels the
variance growth, so the distribution softmax sees stays roughly the same
shape whether the model's key dimension is 8 or 8,000.
</Aside>
</Step>

<Step label="softmax">
## Softmax turns scores into a distribution

Softmax exponentiates every score in a row and divides each by the row's
total, so the row stops being an arbitrary list of numbers and becomes a
proper distribution — non-negative, summing to one. Look at the row for
**it**: essentially all of its mass sits on **dog**.

We didn't arrange that. It's what training produced, and given the training
data, it's close to the only thing training *could* produce: every subject
in that data goes on to share the same verbs and objects, so the only
reliable signal for resolving a pronoun is to look back at the noun that
introduced it.

<Aside kind="deep" title="Now try `cat` in the playground below — and watch it cheat.">
Change the subject to **cat** and the word **it** attends to *itself*, not
to **cat**. This is not a bug, and no amount of additional training fixes
it.

Softmax forces every row to sum to 1, and "put all my mass on myself" is a
*free* code: the value vector at **it** is the same constant in every
sentence, so the layers downstream can simply learn to read that constant
as *purred*. That means the model needs only **two** informative attention
targets to distinguish **three** subjects — one subject can always freeload
on self-attention instead of being looked up at all. In this model, that
freeloader happens to be **cat**.

Real large language models do exactly this, at far greater scale: it is the
mechanism behind *attention sinks*, where a disproportionate share of
attention parks on the first token of a sequence, a token that carries
essentially no information. You are looking at the same phenomenon here, in a
model of two heads and sixteen numbers per word, where it's small enough to
catch in the act.
</Aside>
</Step>

<Step label="A">
## Every word, all at once

**it** is not special. Every word in the sentence holds out a query, every
word's row of scores is scaled, masked, and softmaxed the same way, and the
six rows stack into one grid — the whole attention pattern of this head, on
this sentence, in one picture.

Watch it fill in, a row at a time. Each row is one word's distribution over
what it may look at: the darker the cell, the more of that word's attention
sits there. The staircase along the diagonal is the causal mask made
visible — the first word may attend only to itself, and each word after it
gains exactly one more thing it is allowed to look back at. Nothing may look
forward.

The last row is the one we came for. **it** spends almost all of its
attention on **dog** — and almost none on any of the five other positions
the mask leaves open to it, itself included. That single row is the model
resolving a pronoun — and every number in it is one you have now watched
being built.
</Step>

<Step label="A·V → + x">
## Weighted sum, and we're back where we started

A row of weights on its own changes nothing; it has to be spent. Each word's
output is the weighted average of every word's **value** vector, using the row
you just watched form as the weights. The row for **it** puts nearly all its
weight on **dog**, so the value vector **dog** hands over is very nearly the
whole of what **it** walks away with — the highlighted row above is the one
being copied.

A weighted average of eight-dimensional value vectors is itself
eight-dimensional, so what this head produces is six rows of **eight** numbers —
the shape of the value matrix above it, not of the embeddings we started from.
Getting back to sixteen takes three more moves. The second head has been
computing its own eight numbers per word all along, so the two heads'
outputs are **concatenated** back into sixteen. That sixteen is mixed by one
more matrix, <Equation tex="W_O" />, which lets what one head found inform every
dimension of the result. And the mixed row is **added** onto the row the word
arrived with — the residual connection.

Only now is the shape restored: six rows of sixteen numbers, exactly the
embeddings this page opened with. That's not a coincidence, and it isn't
free — it is bought by the concatenation and by <Equation tex="W_O" /> being
square. It's the entire reason attention blocks can be stacked one after
another, dozens deep, each one reading the previous block's output as if it
were a fresh set of embeddings.
</Step>

<Aside kind="deep" title="This was one head of two, eight numbers wide. Real models run twelve or more, sixty-four wide.">
Every figure on this page was drawn from the first of this model's two heads,
which works on eight numbers per word — half of the sixteen each word arrives
with. The second head ran the whole time, in parallel, on the other eight, with
its own <Equation tex="W_Q" />, <Equation tex="W_K" />, and
<Equation tex="W_V" />, free to learn a different notion of "relevant"; its
output was waiting to be concatenated onto the one you just watched being built.

Production models differ only in how much of this there is. GPT-2 gives each
word 768 numbers and splits them across twelve heads of sixty-four
(<Equation tex="768 / 12 = 64" />) — the same slice, the same dot products, the
same concatenation, the same <Equation tex="W_O" /> mixing the heads back into
one vector per word. The arithmetic doesn't change. There's just more of it,
running side by side.
</Aside>
